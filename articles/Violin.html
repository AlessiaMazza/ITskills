Exploring musical instruments: the violin

This article provides a general insight of a very renowned musical instrument that has marked an important turning point in the history of music : the violin. In particular, we will observe and analyse the main features and the history of this splendid instrument, as well as the most influential and talented violinists that, by virtue of their remarkable works, achieved lasting fame and played a fundamental role in the history of violin music.

In the first step, we proceeded with an exploration of WikiData in order to discover the amount of stringed instruments that were mentioned inside the knowledge base. A stringed instrument can be defined as any musical instrument having strings as the medium of sound production. The violin: an overview

The violin is a bowed string instrument with four strings tuned in perfect fifths. It is the smallest and highest-pitched member of the violin family of string instruments, which also includes the viola, cello and double bass.
A violin is occasionally informally called a fiddle, in spite of what kind of music is being played on it. The word "violin" traces its origins in the Romance languages from the Middle Latin word vitula, meaning "stringed instrument". This word may also be the source of the Germanic "fiddle". A person who plays the violin is called a violinist or fiddler, and a person who produces or repairs them is called a luthier, or simply a violin maker.

The violin has been traditionally employed as an accompaniment to singing and dancing. It is also an important classical solo instrument, as well as taking pride of place at the front of orchestras. The violin is both an object of great beauty and symmetry as well as an ingenious work of mechanical design. George Frideric Handel

George Frideric Handel was a German-British composer and violinist. Born and raised in Halle (Germany), Handel showed interest in musical instruments and composition since the early stages of his life. After receiving his training, he worked as a composer in Hamburg and Italy before settling in London in 1712, where he lived for the rest of his life.

Handel was a composer of the late Baroque era, and he is deemed to be one of the most prominent composers of his time, influencing a whole generation of composers with his unique blend of Italian, German, and English musical styles. Handel’s works spanned a variety of musical genres, including opera, orchestral music, chamber music, and choral works. He is most famous for his oratorios, which are large-scale musical works for choirs, soloists, and orchestras.

Handel’s musical style was heavily influenced by the Italian Baroque style, which he encountered during his time in Italy. He incorporated elements of the Italian style into his own compositions, blending them with elements of German and English musical traditions. Handel's musical style was influential in his time and it continues to be celebrated today for its combination of beauty, grandeur and emotional depth. His works are considered milestones of the Baroque era and they are an enduring testament to his mastery of the musical arts.
